AdvReverb: Rethinking the Stealthiness of Audio Adversarial Examples to Human Perception

Published on November 25, 2023 by Meng Chen Under Minor Revision

Abstract: As one of the most representative applications built on deep learning, audio systems, including keyword spotting, automatic speech recognition and speaker identification, have recently been demonstrated to suffer from the vulnerability to adversarial examples, which have already raised general concerns in both academia and industry. Existing attacks follow the same adversarial example generation approach from computer vision, i.e., overlaying the optimized additive perturbations on original voices. However, due to the additive perturbations’ nature on human audibility, balancing the stealthiness and attack capability remains a challenging problem. In this paper, we rethink the stealthiness of audio adversarial examples, and turn to introduce another kind of audio distortions, i.e., the reverberation, as a new perturbation format for stealthy adversarial example generation. Such a convolutional adversarial perturbations are optimized to approximate real-world impulse responses for imitating natural reverberation so as to deceive human ears. Based on this idea, we propose AdvReverb to construct, optimize, and deliver phoneme-level convolutional adversarial perturbations with both speech and music carriers. Experimental results demonstrate that AdvReverb could realize high attack success rates over 95% on three audio-domain tasks while achieving superior perceptual quality and keeping stealthy from human perception in both over-the-air and over-the-line delivery scenarios.

Index terms: Convolutional adversarial example, automatic audio system, impulse response, stealthy adversarial perturbation

Open-source code: AdvReverb Repo

Threat Model

We consider both digital and physical attack scenarios where the adversary delivers the adversarial example over the line or over the air. In the over-the-air delivery, the adversary physically approaches deployed audio systems and uses a speaker device to emit adversarial examples. In this case, the adversary suffers from exposure by human listeners in the vicinity of the audio system. In the over-the-line delivery, the adversary directly injects adversarial examples into audio systems through digital access, e.g., online API query. Such an attack runs the risk of exposure by human inspectors.

In both scenarios, it is necessary for the adversary to ensure the perceptual stealthiness of audio adversarial examples.

Stealthy Audio Adversarial Example

From Additive to Convolutional

Existing attacks generate unnoticeble audio adversarial examples by adding subtle adversarial perturbations $\delta$ on the original sample $x$ and restricting their amplitude or frequency through:

  • $L_\infty$ Norm: $\mathop{\arg\min}\limits_{\delta} L(F(x+\delta),y_t), s.t. \Vert \delta \Vert_{\infty} \le \epsilon.$
  • $L_2$ Penalty: $\mathop{\arg\min}\limits_{\delta} L(F(x+\delta),y_t) + \alpha \Vert \delta \Vert_{2}.$
  • PsychoMask: $\mathop{\arg\min}\limits_{\delta} L(F(x+\delta),y_t) + \alpha \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor\frac{N}{2}\rfloor}\max(S_{\delta}(k)-H_x(k),0).$

However, current additive adversarial perturbations suffer from attack exposure due to their limited stealthiness. Amplitude normalization and penaly induce obvious noises and artifacts, while frequency masking fails to hide perturbations in the inaudible domain under the defense of Dompteur, making additive perturbations easily perceptible.

To bridge this gap, we propose a new class of audio adversarial example for stealthy attacks. Considering the inevitable sound channel distortion for normal speech in both over-the-air and over-the-line scenario, we propose to disguise perturbation-induced distortion as natural channel distortion for deceiving human listeners and inspectors. Different from existing additive adversarial example, i.e., $x’=x+\delta$, we turn to derive the adversarial example by $x’=x*r’$, here $r’$ is called convolutional adversarial perturbation. During the optimization, we reshape the adversarial perturbation $r’$ to approximate a realistic impulse response $r$ for imitating natural reverberation.

According to both the objective evaluation and subjective listening test, we can observe that:

  • Amplitude constraints (i.e., $L_\infty$ norm and $L_2$ penalty) still induces full-band noises or high-frequency irregular artifacts, which is obviously noticeble to human so as to cause attack exposure.
  • Frequency constraint (i.e., PsychoMask) can generate nearly imperceptible adversarial perturbations under the hearing threshold. However, such perturbations are easily filtered out by simple signal processing methods, e.g., Dompteur. Although the adaptive attack that considers Dompteur can still succeed, it has to introduce noticeble artifacts, also leading to attack exposure.
  • Different from previous additive attacks, the convolutional adversarial examples generated by AdvReverb sounds similar to the over-the-air samples, which involve natural reverberation during physical propagation, even under the defense of Dompteur. AdvReverb improves the attack stealthiness since it is difficult for human to distinguish the reverberation and convolutional adversarial perturbation.
